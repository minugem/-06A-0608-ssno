Introduction

With computers being further implemented in education and learning, there has been an increasing rise in the usage of the flipped classroom model in higher education. The flipped classroom model is a “student-centred learning method” (Ozdamli & Asiksoy, 2016, p. 99) that is commonly structured by students watching lecture recordings at home and completing typical homework in class. This results in direct instruction not being conducted in large groups, rather, they are delivered individually, often through teacher-created videos (Bergmann & Sams, 2014). Students are expected to prepare by studying before class, giving students the opportunity to use this knowledge inside of the classroom with the guidance of a teacher during the course (Ozdamli & Asiksoy, 2016), resulting in interactive, collaborative and problem-solving activities to be carried out inside of the classroom (Cabı, 2018). As Bergmann & Sams (2014) said, “Flipped learning, at its core, is individualized learning” (p. 7). As a result of this, the utilisation of this method of learning greatly affects how a student will learn, with several studies researching the benefits and drawbacks of the flipped learning model on a students education.

Opportunities

Ahmed (2016) points out how, despite teachers being aware that students do not understand the material they are teaching, there is not enough time in a traditional classroom model for teachers to assist each student individually in the class period without falling behind schedule. This teaching style is teacher-centred, with no differentiation between the way students are taught. As a result, students are passive learners, with teachers taking control of all responsibility for the students' learning (Bergmann & Sams, 2014). The flipped classroom learning model directly combats this by utilising technology to grant the teacher more time to interact with students instead of lecturing. This allows for teachers to prioritise creating opportunities for students (Yungwei, 2016) with “student-centred strategies, approaches, and curricula to provide students with dynamic and innovative opportunities for their learning.“ (Brewer, 2018, p. 409). This model of learning has several benefits for the student, such as “more one on one time with students, opportunities for active and collaborative learning, missed lectures, self-paced learning, and ‘‘just-in-time’’ type instruction” (Roach, 2014, p. 75). Below is a diagram from Ahemd (2016) detailing the differences between the traditional and flipped classroom methods.

The utilisation of the flipped learning model presents students with a plethora of opportunities throughout their education. Students are able to gain exposure to pre-class materials through video recordings. This blended learning environment allows for students to gain more control over how their time is spent, allowing for the student to take responsibility in regards to their education. This has an added benefit for these students by allowing students who are falling behind to rewatch past video recordings (Ahmed, 2016). A survey regarding student engagement performed by Fisher et al. (2017) showed that students strongly felt that they were in control of their learning, crediting the flipped learning model to this. This model of learning ultimately results in students becoming more independent. Students had found that the control they had over their learning and time led to an overall positive learning experience. Furthermore, survey participants noted that the ability to control their time granted several positive benefits by allowing a greater flexibility in regards to their timetable, allowing for the student to better balance their personal and educational lives. Additionally, Cabı (2018) reported that students autonomously studying and subsequently reaching their academic goals raised students’ self-confidence. Students ultimately gain control of their education, under this learning model they are granted a choice in what content they engage in and when (Brewer, 2018). The control students had over their learning served as training for self-directed learning, allowing for students to further develop their self-discipline skills (Yungwei, 2016).

Teachers are able to utilise the preparation students perform before the class to then assist and build off of the knowledge the student possesses. Students are able to get individualised support as a result of this, getting assistance for the level of knowledge they have (Brewer, 2018). The pre-preparation for class also adds to increased teamwork skills, as students won’t spend the entire class time focusing on learning new material and can instead work with their peers more effectively and enhance both of their understandings on the given topic (Du et al., 2014). Yungwei (2016) brings attention to how the lack of formal lectures allows for students to discuss opinions with one another, with Ahmed (2016) adding to this my mentioning that the flipped classroom model lengthens the amount of time students can interact with their peers as well as with their teachers. This promotes inquiry-based learning, as students are able to more directly ask questions and get responses.

The use of electronics in flipped classrooms grants students the opportunities to learn anywhere and at any time. Fisher et al. (2017) points out that with this advantage, students who are under time constraints are able to use their mobile devices to access and engage in course material anywhere, allowing for them to increase their time management skills. This allows for remote learning to occur if the individual is ill or otherwise unable to arrive to class (Birgili et al., 2021). Another advantage with the implementation of technology that Du et al. (2014) elaborates on is the use to rewatch videos repeatedly. They point out that students in a traditional classroom may struggle to transcribe the information given to them from a teacher, however, with the use of video, students are able to freely rewind and fast-forwards when necessary. Birgili et al. (2021) makes an interesting point regarding the benefit of utilising technology in this learning model, as using videos to teach Generation Z can increase the appeal and efficiency of learning. They also mention how visual ads “help students to have an integral outlook as well as increase their commitment to the class and enhance their ability to transfer acquired knowledge to other topics” (p. 386).

Flipped classrooms' biggest strength is its personalisation. Students are able to adjust the way they learn to meet their needs, such as if they have a preference for being in an auditory or visual medium and if they perform better in the morning or afternoon. Every student has an individual learning style and a flipped classroom is able to more adequately cater to that (Ahmed, 2016). This model of learning encourages students to develop their critical thinking, collaboration, communications (Brewer, 2018) and problem-solving skills (Birgili et al., 2021), as well as encouraging “social interaction, teamwork and cultural diversity among students” (Du et al., 2014, p. 18) by requiring students to collaborate with one another. Flipped learning allows for a personalised education to fit each individual student's needs, granting each student the opportunity to learn at a pace that suits them and ultimately results in students “engaging with content, engaging with their classmates, and taking control over their own learning” (Ahmed, 2016, p.432).

Risks

The flipped classroom model isn’t without its risks towards students, as depending on the individual, many of the opportunities this learning model presents can also be drawbacks. Cabı (2018) states that the problems regarding this learning model can be “categorized under three main titles: Motivation, Content, and Learning” (p. 216), providing a table showing their results when they asked students what problems they encountered.

The flipped learning model has a heavy reliance on student participation in their own time, as a result it becomes the students responsibility to view the material before their class. However, students' learning will be impacted by time constraints limiting the amount of time they may dedicate to studying, directly impacting student preparedness (Cabı, 2018). Additionally, students may be unable to properly balance the amount of time dedicated to each class, as some subjects may be too complicated to complete online due to the content being taught being far too long and intensive (Fisher et al., 2017) and as a result they may spend more time on these courses than they would have in a traditional classroom model (Yungwei, 2016). Less motivated students will also interact less with their pre-class material, inevitably get less work completed, as well as they are likely to procrastinate when it comes to completing pre-course material, all of which will impact their learning inside of the classroom (Du et al., 2014). Both Ozdamli & Asiksoy (2016) and Birgili et al. (2021) point out that if students do not interact with the pre-class material, it results in instructors being unable to proceed with the course material and students being unable to follow material inside of the classroom. Furthermore, Yungwei (2016) brings attention to how students' lack of preparedness doesn’t just affect the individual, but any group they are assigned in for class work, resulting in students who had prepared for the class doing more work than they should have and an overall lack of teamwork in the classroom. Similarly, Fisher et al. (2017) mentions how the lack of constantly being told what to do left students with low self-motivation to not improve their study habits. Zhu et al. (2022) adds to this by pointing out how this low engagement with the course material negatively impacts overall academic performance of the individual. Student’s education and overall achievement is dependent on their participation as “Any tendency of learners to refuse to take part in lesson preparation, may result in a negative effect on their academic performance” (Birgili et al. 2021, p. 370).

Cabı (2018) brings attention to the difficulty students experience when studying outside of the classroom, as well as the difficulty some students may experience when attempting to understand the course material. Due to the less formal environment, students are discouraged from studying course material. Likewise, Yungwei (2012) includes students hesitancy to learn under the flipped classroom model, stating that the course work was difficult to comprehend and that some cannot understand the content they have read, rather preferring to have an instructor teach them instead. Similarly, Cabı (2018) also found that some students would prefer to learn inside of the classroom with a video recording to watch later if needed due to the inability to concentrate on studying on their own.

As Birgili et al. (2021) points out, video quality can make a huge impact on a students learning experience. If the videos are too long or of a poor quality, students may be negatively impacted. Additionally, Zhu et al. (2022) adds that videos over 50 minutes in length tended not to be attractive to students. Another drawback of the pre-class work being online is that students are required to sit in front of a computer screen, ultimately increasing the amount of screentime a student gets (Du et al., 2014). Another struggle students may experience due to technology is the distraction they cause. Both Du et al. (2014) and Fisher et al. (2017) point out that the presence of technology, including social media, television and computers, distracts students from completing their class work.

It is important to also recognise that this model of learning cannot be applied to everybody and that individual differences need to be considered (Yungwei, 2016). Fisher et al. (2017) found that students commonly found trouble adjusting to the flipped learning model, ultimately creating a significant learning curb in the beginning of the semester due to issues understanding how the learning process was structured. Yungwei (2016) also found that many students were not prepared for the responsibility that comes with taking control of your learning, and students lacking readiness when it comes to self-directed learning had difficulty staying on track with the course materials. One of flipped learning's biggest advantages, the ability for students to personalise their learning experience, can also be one of the largest risks as students may not be prepared for the responsibilities.

Choices

With the flipped learning models common implementation around the globe, several questions are raised surrounding the choices provided by using this model of learning. The flipped learning model is described as being a universal-design component by Sullivan (2018), this means it is to be usable, accessible and inclusive for everyone without the need for adaptation or specialised design (Burgstahler, 2021). However, Sullivan (2018) notes that providers of flipped learning need to consider how using this model impacts the barriers to access, as students with chronic illnesses that prevent them from attending class may cause challenges, as attendance to classes is crucial when using this learning model. While flipped learning does ultimately connect more people to education in an easy fashion, individual circumstances still need to be considered as they can still prevent people from utilising this model of learning.

Flipped learning is fundamentally linked to technology, to the point that it cannot happen without it (Birgili et al., 2021). As such, the lack of proper access to equipment that extraordinarily affects a students education. Ozdamli & Asiksoy (2016) mentions how student’s learning can be impacted by the lack of equipment, such as lacking a computer or having difficulty connecting and accessing the internet. Similarly, Du et al. (2014) focuses on how students, specifically those from lower income backgrounds, may not be able to purchase their own computers and may not have access to the internet, which is required to participate in a flipped classroom. The students whose access to the internet fluctuated are put at a disadvantage compared to their peers. They further point out how these students will be forced to use public computers or networks, from places such as a library, which can become a problem if they are unable to access these resources.

Flipped learning primarily utilises online lectures and content to deliver course material to students, which can lead to a lack of personalisation in the content given. As Sullivan (2018) points out, if these lectures are not accessible by adaptive software, students with visual, learning or auditory processing disabilities are disadvantaged compared to their peers. This brings attention to the importance of lectures and course coordinators addressing the individual needs of students and adapting accordingly to them, allowing for everybody to engage in the course content equally. Similarly, Chen & Hsu (2022) recognised that the flipped learning model fell short where in the areas lacking variation to fit the individual needs of the students. Furthermore, Chen & Hsu (2022) conducted a study on flipped learning and if adaptive instructions and teaching techniques could improve a students education. Adaptive teaching helps make up for the shortcomings flipped learning presents by customising to the students needs. As AI grows in popularity, it becomes increasingly relevant when considering flipped learning, specifically when AI is implemented in chatbots. A review by Lo & Hew (2023) had found that AI was being designed and trained as a learning chatbot that provided students with real-time personalised feedback on the students performance and answered questions asked by the students. Additionally, it was found that an assisting chatbot could provide students resources for the student to utilise as pre-class material and that a mentoring chatbot could assist students in creating learning goals and provide advice on how they may achieve these goals. These chatbots granted students with immediate feedback for their studies, increased class preparedness, student confidence, and engagement with the course content. However, the implementation of AI chatbots in flipped learning isn’t without challenges. These chatbots were shown to be limited in their technical functionality, as they may be inadequate in providing to the students' needs, and presented a lack of authenticity when answering students' questions. Chatbots are an extremely recent technology that has seen a surge in its use and advancements. Whilst it has been shown to provide significant benefits to students in helping further their knowledge, there are still several limitations that the AI faces which can ultimately only be resolved by humans.

Conclusion

As computers are becoming progressively more entwined with education and learning, it has become increasingly important to understand the impacts of the flipped learning model on students and their education. Flipped learning presents students with a plethora of opportunities throughout their education. This model of learning is student-centred, this leaves students in control of their education, ultimately increasing students self-confidence, responsibility and various other personal skills. As a result, this grants students with a large amount of personalisation when studying, allowing for them to choose when, where and what to study. Furthermore, students are able to access course material through their devices, allowing for them to learn anywhere. However, flipped learning is not without its risks, as plenty of the opportunities flipped learning presents can backfire on the student. As a result of students being in control of their studies, students with little motivation and poor time management skills may ignore the course materials, ultimately having a negative effect on their academic performance. Furthermore, if one student's neglect of their study doesn’t just affect them, it also affects the students around them. In group activities, the other team members will end up having to take on more work than they should have. Subject complexity is another concern, as the self-directed learning may make it difficult for students who lack understanding in the course material to study and further their education. The flipped learning model does provide a plethora of choices for students. The lack of access to the internet and computers can significantly impact a student's education, as well as the personalisation of education material to cater to individuals needs. Additionally, the rise in AI has become increasingly relevant in the discussions around flipped learning, as chatbots made with AI can greatly impact a students studies. Ultimately, the success of the flipped learning model depends on the individual. Whilst it is pertinent to take the individual into consideration as the success of this model highly depends on the individual, flipped learning allows for students to have a greater control and far more personalisation than they would have under a traditional learning model.
